The problem

Perishable inventory fails in two directions, silently

Shelf life across this dataset runs 15 to 72 days. That window is short enough that a manual weekly review catches a failure only after it's already happened — in either direction.

Failure mode 1

Stockout, discovered late

Demand for a product-region pair drifts upward. Because the series is thin — often under 30 recorded transactions across four years — nobody has enough history in that one segment to see it coming until stock is already gone.

Failure mode 2

Overproduction, written off

A safe-looking flat buffer overshoots real demand. For a product with a two-week shelf life, that surplus doesn't sit in a warehouse waiting for next quarter — it's discarded.

Both get filed under "inventory management" today, using the same flat minimum-stock threshold regardless of how much history actually supports it. Neither gets a forecast with honest uncertainty attached.

How it works

Four stages, one honest number at the end

The pipeline runs offline once against the full transaction history, then the console reads its output — no model calls at demo time.

01

Data layer

4,325 transactions, 4 years, 10 products, 15 states — a median of roughly 28 records per product-region pair.

02

Pooling

A hierarchical Bayesian model (PyMC, NUTS) shares statistical strength across products and regions — a thin series borrows from the group without being flattened into it.

03

Inventory logic

Forecast uncertainty converts directly into safety stock and reorder point — not a flat buffer applied everywhere the same way.

04

Recommendation

A reorder quantity and risk flag per product-region, checked against the threshold already on file.

Why pooling, not one model per segment

Each thin, noisy line on the left is a real product-region history — too short to model alone with any confidence. Partial pooling lets every segment borrow strength from the product and region it belongs to, without forcing all segments to the same average.

Explainability

Every forecast shows its own uncertainty, not just a number

The same driver view answers "why is this reorder urgent" and "why is this one fine" — read the same bars either direction.

A flat reorder threshold treats a product with 200 recorded transactions the same as one with 12. This model doesn't — every recommendation carries the width of its own credible interval, so a thin segment gets a wider safety margin instead of a false sense of precision.

That's also what the console's driver view is for: not a black-box score, but the specific reasons — seasonality, base rate, how much the estimate leaned on the group versus its own history — behind each number.
